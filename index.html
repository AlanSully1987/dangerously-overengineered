Fire Hazards Hidden Inside Modern Power Infrastructure

By Alan O'Sullivan

Modern fire suppression design starts with understanding the actual risks inside the power spine of a building. Below is a breakdown of the key components that keep a facility alive and the real fire hazards hiding within them.

Transformers

Transformers handle enormous electrical and thermal stress. They carry high volumes of oil, polymer insulation, windings and high-voltage connections. When they fail, they fail fast.

  • Internal electrical arcing
  • Oil spray ignition
  • Pool fires from ruptured tanks
  • Cable insulation ignition

Electrical Rooms

These rooms are the control centres for power distribution, and their fire hazards reflect the huge concentrations of cabling and switchgear inside them.

  • Arc flash events
  • Overheated terminations
  • Tracking faults across dust or moisture
  • Cable tray ignition

UPS Rooms

UPS systems run continuously and support the load during power loss, but their power electronics and high DC currents introduce specific fire risks.

  • Thermal failure of modules
  • DC arcing
  • Overheated plastics
  • Internal wiring faults

Battery Rooms

Battery rooms store vast amounts of chemical and electrical energy, making them some of the highest-risk spaces in a building’s infrastructure.

Lead-Acid Batteries

  • Hydrogen build-up
  • Spark ignition
  • Thermal buildup in cell banks
  • High-current DC arcs

Lithium-Ion Batteries

  • Thermal runaway
  • Flammable gas venting
  • Jet flame behaviour
  • Re-ignition and propagation

Generators with Belly Tanks

Outdoor generators are self-contained but still high-risk due to fuel, heat and electrics in one enclosure.

  • Diesel pool fires
  • Pressurised spray fires
  • Oil mist on hot exhaust surfaces
  • Electrical faults in control panels
